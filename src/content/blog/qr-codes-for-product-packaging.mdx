## Why QR Codes Belong on Your Packaging

Every product you sell has a limited amount of physical space. The label can hold a name, a logo, regulatory text, and maybe a tagline. That is it.

A QR code expands that space infinitely. A single 2 cm square on your packaging can link to:

- **Ingredient lists and allergen information** — legally required in many categories, and often too detailed for a small label
- **How-to-use videos** — show customers how to assemble, prepare, or apply your product
- **Warranty registration** — capture customer data and activate warranties with a single scan
- **Authenticity verification** — let customers confirm they bought a genuine product
- **Sustainability info** — share your sourcing story, carbon footprint, or recycling instructions
- **Reorder page** — one scan to repurchase, reducing friction in the repeat buying cycle

This is what "smart packaging" means in practice — a physical product that connects to a digital experience.

## Industries Using QR Codes on Packaging

### Food and beverage

Nutritional details, recipe ideas, sourcing stories, batch traceability. Indian FSSAI regulations increasingly encourage digital labelling for supplementary information.

### Cosmetics and personal care

Full ingredient lists (INCI names), application tutorials, skin type quizzes, and review pages. Particularly useful for products with small packaging.

### Pharmaceuticals

Dosage instructions, drug interaction warnings, authentication, and expiry tracking. Serialisation requirements in many markets already mandate unique identifiers on packaging.

### Electronics

Setup guides, firmware updates, warranty registration, and customer support portals. A QR code replaces the thick instruction manual nobody reads.

### Fashion and apparel

Care instructions, size guides, styling lookbooks, and sustainability certifications. Brands like Pangaia and Everlane use QR codes to share their supply chain transparency.

## Best Practices for QR Codes on Packaging

### Placement

- Place the QR code where customers naturally look — near the product name, on the back panel, or next to the barcode
- Avoid placing it on curved surfaces where distortion makes scanning difficult
- On flexible packaging (pouches, bags), position the QR code on the flattest panel

### Size

- **Minimum 15mm × 15mm** for close-range scanning (held in hand)
- **20mm × 20mm or larger** is recommended for reliability
- If the package is typically scanned on a shelf (not in hand), go bigger — 25mm+

### Quiet zone

Keep at least 4 modules of white space around the QR code. Do not let design elements bleed into this zone.

### Call-to-action

Always tell the customer why they should scan:

- "Scan for full ingredients"
- "Watch the setup video"
- "Register your warranty"
- "Verify authenticity"

Without context, most consumers will ignore the QR code entirely.

### Print quality

QR codes on packaging must survive the printing process — offset, flexo, or digital. Key considerations:

- **Minimum module size of 0.5mm** for flexo printing; 0.3mm for digital and offset
- **High contrast** between module colour and background
- **Test on the actual substrate** — matte, gloss, kraft, and metallic surfaces all behave differently
- Export your QR code as **SVG** for vector-perfect reproduction at any size
