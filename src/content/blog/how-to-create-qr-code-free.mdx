## What Is a QR Code?

A QR (Quick Response) code is a two-dimensional barcode that stores information — a URL, contact details, WiFi credentials, or plain text — in a pattern of black and white squares. Anyone with a smartphone camera can scan it instantly, making QR codes one of the simplest bridges between the physical and digital world.

## Why Use a QR Code?

QR codes have moved well beyond warehouse logistics. Today, they show up on business cards, restaurant tables, product packaging, event badges, and storefront windows. The reasons are straightforward:

- **Instant access** — no typing URLs or searching
- **Trackable** — know how many people scanned and when
- **Versatile** — link to websites, PDFs, videos, payment pages, or app downloads
- **Cost-free** — generating a basic QR code costs nothing

## Tips for Getting the Most Out of Your QR Code

### Keep the destination mobile-friendly

Over 90% of QR code scans happen on smartphones. If the page your QR code points to is not mobile-optimised, you are wasting the scan.

### Test before printing

Always scan the QR code yourself — on multiple devices — before sending it to print. A mistyped URL is an expensive mistake on 5,000 business cards.

### Add a short call-to-action

A QR code on its own is a mystery. Pair it with a line like "Scan for menu" or "Save my contact" so people know what to expect.

### Use high contrast colours

QR codes need contrast to scan reliably. Dark modules on a light background works best. If you use brand colours, test scannability before committing to a design.

### Choose SVG for print

If the QR code will appear on printed material — flyers, packaging, banners — export it as SVG. Vector formats scale to any size without losing sharpness.
